# AI Policy Brief — March 6, 2026
**Topic:** General AI Policy Scan
**Sources:** CBC, Globe and Mail, BetaKit, The Logic

---

## Top Takeaways

1. **Tumbler Ridge shooting puts AI safety regulation front and centre** — An 18-year-old who killed eight people in B.C. had been flagged and banned by ChatGPT months earlier for violent posts, but OpenAI chose not to alert police. Canada has no law requiring AI companies to report dangerous users. AI Minister Evan Solomon is now demanding a safety plan from OpenAI and meeting with CEO Sam Altman.

2. **Ottawa is scrapping the old AI law and starting fresh** — The Liberals won't reintroduce AIDA (the Artificial Intelligence and Data Act). Instead, Minister Solomon is building a new regulatory framework focused on copyright and safety. He says creators deserve compensation when AI trains on their work, but the details are still being worked out.

3. **Canada's $2B "sovereign AI" plan may mostly benefit American companies** — The federal government committed $2 billion to build Canadian AI compute infrastructure, but OpenAI and other U.S. firms are aggressively lobbying for the contracts. Critics warn the money could end up subsidizing foreign tech giants rather than building real domestic capability.

4. **Provinces aren't waiting for Ottawa** — Manitoba launched public consultations on AI regulation, exploring age limits for AI access and requiring companies to get consent before using personal data. With no federal AI law in place, provinces are starting to fill the gap on their own.

5. **AI literacy is now a hiring requirement at major Canadian banks** — BMO and CIBC expect all new hires to be proficient with AI tools. BMO's chief learning officer called AI "a core skill for anyone we are bringing into the institution." Google is also putting $13M into Canadian AI workforce development.

## What to Watch
- Solomon's meeting with Sam Altman — will signal how tough Canada gets on AI safety
- New AI regulatory framework expected later in 2026
- Manitoba consultation outcomes could set a precedent for other provinces
