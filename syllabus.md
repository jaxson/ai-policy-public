## Course Description

This interdisciplinary graduate seminar provides a practical and critical introduction to artificial intelligence (AI) systems and their governance. Designed for future policy leaders, technologists, and analysts, the course blends technical foundations with real-world policy applications to equip students with the knowledge and tools needed to engage with AI at both strategic and operational levels.

Students will learn how modern machine learning models work, explore the global AI supply chain (including chips, data, and compute infrastructure), and use contemporary AI tools to support their own policy research and analysis. The course covers major policy and governance issues including AI safety and frontier model red-teaming, AI security, responsible AI, copyright and intellectual property, international regulatory approaches, platform governance, and a variety of different approaches and policy frameworks.

Each week combines conceptual learning, real-world case studies, and applied exercises. The course will feature industry leaders as regular guest speakers. Students will gain experience using generative AI, working with datasets, and evaluating AI systems. Final projects will allow students to develop applied policy interventions or technical prototypes that address contemporary challenges in AI governance.

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this course, students will have a strong, practical foundation in how modern AI systems work and how they shape public policy in Canada and around the world. Students will develop the ability to cut through the hype, understand what's actually happening under the hood, and evaluate how emerging technologies intersect with governance, institutions, and society. More specifically, students will be able to:

1. **Understand how contemporary machine learning systems are built, deployed, and governed.**
   Students will gain a working understanding of the technical stack — data, models, chips, compute, and infrastructure — along with the governance frameworks shaping them. The goal isn't to turn students into engineers, but to make sure they can speak fluently with technologists, interpret technical claims, and form policy positions grounded in reality.

2. **Analyze the societal, economic, and geopolitical implications of AI across sectors and jurisdictions.**
   Students will examine real-world cases — from healthcare to education, national security to the global AI race — and learn how AI is reshaping institutions, markets, public trust, and international cooperation. They'll be equipped to assess risks, opportunities, and trade-offs with a public-interest lens.

3. **Design thoughtful policy and governance responses that reflect both technical realities and societal values.**
   Students will learn to craft policy interventions, governance mechanisms, and institutional strategies that can actually work — in government, in industry, and across sectors. We'll explore what responsible AI looks like in practice and how policymakers can build systems that earn public confidence.

4. **Use modern AI tools in ways that are directly relevant to careers in public service.**
   Students will gain hands-on experience with generative AI, analytical tools, and structured evaluation methods. They'll learn how to use these systems to accelerate research, draft and analyze policy options, model scenarios, and communicate clearly with stakeholders. No coding is required — just curiosity and a willingness to experiment.

## Course Format

This course is taught in person and works best when students show up ready to engage. Each week blends seminar-style discussions with practical examples and frequently lectures from guest speakers. This will help students build both a conceptual understanding of AI systems and the practical skills needed to work with them in real policy environments.

We'll welcome leaders from government, industry, and academia who are shaping the development of AI around the world. Their experiences will give real-world context to the concepts discussed in class and also allow students to gain a window into prospective career opportunities in this field.

Because this is a participatory seminar, you are expected to complete the recommended readings, explore the AI tools introduced, and come prepared to contribute thoughtful questions and insights. This is not a passive course. Our goal is to learn from one another, challenge assumptions, and connect technical ideas with the kinds of policy choices public servants, analysts, and leaders face every day.

Throughout the term, you'll work individually and in small groups and are encouraged to experiment with AI tools and leverage them to complete assignments that mimic how AI will be used in policy work environments. The course culminates in a final applied project and presentation.

## Evaluation Criteria

### Briefing Note 1 (10%)

Write a 1,500-word briefing note on a topic from Sessions 1–3 (i.e., AI basics, LLMs, compute infrastructure). Target audience: a Deputy Minister unfamiliar with the technical details. The note should explain the topic clearly, identify key policy implications for Canada, and offer preliminary recommendations.

**Graded on:**
- Clarity of explanation for a non-technical audience
- Technical accuracy
- Policy relevance and actionable framing
- Professional writing and structure

### Briefing Note 2 (20%)

Write a 2,500-word briefing note on a topic or a couple of topics from Sessions 4–6 (i.e., AI ethics and governance, large-scale digital transformation, and AI safety). Must engage substantively with at least two course readings and incorporate a Canadian policy lens. This note should go beyond description to offer analysis and concrete recommendations.

**Graded on:**
- Quality of argument and analysis
- Engagement with course materials and external sources
- Actionable and realistic recommendations
- Canadian policy relevance

### Detailed Policy Proposal and/or AI Policy Agent (25%)

Students should form groups of 3 or 4 and may choose one of two tracks:

**Track A — Policy Intervention Memo:**
A 4,000-word policy intervention memo addressing a specific AI governance challenge in Canada. Must include: problem definition, stakeholder analysis, options analysis (minimum three options), recommended course of action, an implementation roadmap, and risk assessment.

**Track B — Technical Prototype:**
A functional AI agent or tool prototype designed for a public-sector use case, accompanied by a 2,000-word design rationale explaining the problem addressed, technical approach, governance considerations, and limitations.

**Graded on:**
- Depth of analysis or technical sophistication
- Feasibility and realism of proposal
- Integration of course concepts
- Quality of writing or technical execution

### Oral Presentation (25%)

A 15–20 minute presentation, in groups of 3 or 4. Present either a policy proposal and/or AI agent prototype to class. This will be evaluated by the instructor as well as potentially a panel of evaluators.

**Graded on:**
- Clarity and structure of presentation
- Quality of argumentation and evidence
- Ability to handle Q&A from evaluators
- Professional delivery and time management

### Participation (20%)

Active engagement in seminar discussions, guest speaker Q&A, and in-class exercises. This is a series of participatory seminars — you are expected to come prepared, having completed (at least some) of the recommended readings, and ready to contribute thoughtful questions and insights.

**Assessed on:**
- Quality and frequency of contributions to class discussion
- Engagement with guest speakers
- Participation in in-class exercises and workshops
- Demonstrated preparation (having done the readings)

Attendance is expected. Regular absences will likely affect this grade.

### Late Penalty

You have a **one-week window** on Assignments 1 & 2, although I'd recommend sending work in on time. You do not need to notify me that it will be late. Typically, assignments received more than a week after the deadline will be deducted a full letter grade (i.e., from an A to a B) per week.

The final assignment (the policy proposal or AI agent) will have a **3-day window** after which a full letter grade will be deducted per day of delay.

The culminating oral presentation is an alternative to an exam and so must be delivered on time, or else face an immediate full letter grade deduction per day of delay.

If you have significant medical or other extenuating personal circumstances, please email me at jaxson.khan@utoronto.ca. Further notes and official UofT policy for accessibility services are available below.

### Generative AI Policy

*Official UofT policy is below — however, I encourage you to use AI regularly and frequently to advance your research and work. My own process is usually to outline manually, use AI to flesh it out, then edit and iterate — but the final steps and verification should always be human-led.*

*The most important part is to verify every one of your sources (as AI sometimes makes them up) and ensure that you are not using AI to copy and paste / plagiarize the work of others.*

*If your work reads as very obviously, comprehensively written by AI, I will factor that into grading and may follow up with you for an in-person interview to ask you more about your understanding of the assignment.*

*Remember, the point of the course is to learn — you won't get the same chance in the workplace.*

**Out-of-Class Assignments — Outlining:**
By default, students may use AI-generated outlines from ChatGPT, GPT-enabled Bing, or other approved software in preparation for writing a paper or essay. This can be a useful way to begin the writing process. Students should submit, as an appendix to any paper produced from an outline in this manner, a copy of their interaction with the AI tool.

**Out-of-Class Assignments — Completing:**
By default, students may not use generative AI to produce completed drafts of papers, mathematical analyses, or other submittable work. This includes text, images, audio, and video. Doing so will constitute a serious violation of the Code of Behaviour on Academic Matters and will trigger an investigation under the Code. Penalties for this use can range from a reprimand to expulsion from the program.
